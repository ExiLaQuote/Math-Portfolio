Statistical Application

One afternoon I contemplated personality tests. Was the MBTI reliable? So having gone through Module 2 about mean and variance, as well as remembering my basic statistics, I set out to test my conjecture: that personality tests must be determined across several days, since there is inherent instability when describing one's self in a single disposition of emotions and a mere single moment. Taking the test after a family fight will yield a different personality than if I take it after calling with my crush.

Interpreting Results


Essentially I grouped together all the 5 values from 5 days for each personality aspect. I chose the left traits, meaning if it's below 50% then I possess more of the opposite trait. Note that every time before taking the MBTI, I wrote down my mood in that very moment. So, after all these values I could average out each column to attain my "true" or at least more reliable personality. Behold, it ended up being fairly consistent with the personalities that constituted that final result. So sure, it's clear that the standard deviation must not be too wide then. However, it was far more interesting to analyze how the values varied under which trait.


I found out that my Extroversion and Assertiveness were most subject to change, which we would expect since they are influenced by external forces. Nevertheless, I was ENFP-A, with T for thinking versus F feeling being 50%-50%. I was pleased with the result, even if contradicted my initial proposition. Personality was in fact not as volatile as one might think it is. I recommend anyone else to try this for themselves, to find out their weighted personality as well as which traits are most susceptible to changing. But I was also delighted that I was able to use statistics and a statistician's mindset in the real world, in an experiment not often done at all.

GROUPWORK

While it might make sense to distribute and divide the work among our team into the different lesson sections, I operated under a strict principle. This principle was "independent cross-referencing." I believe in checking each other's work by doing your own work. If they were to help each other, they would either make the same mistake, or by too shy to try a different method entirely. I needed independent sources to corroborate into our final answer. In this way, the majority wins, yet we would make sure we knew why the minority's answers were flawed.

VERIFICATION

As the group leader I tasked myself with going over all their answers one-by-one, person-by-person and input their answers into a spreadsheet. Here we could easily compare our answers and see if most of us thought of the same thing. I didn't release this sheet so that they wouldn't see others' answers and rely on those or be shamed because their answers were far different.

TEAM MORALE


I would also make sure that they feel praised and acknowledged. I do this consciously, maturely, and intentionally. No one’s contributions were overlooked or forgotten. I'd say we all did our part.


I genuinely meant it. I love my group, and they respect me as the leader. They were always willing to listen and correct the errors I point out, but they were also brave to question my solution and propose other ways to do things. This goes for the final solution formatting, the way we divided the questions, and if I made a careless mistake.

LIFE LESSONS


I have learned two lessons that I intend on holding close to my heart moving forward in academics and in life. First, on the individual level, don’t underestimate the simplest lesson. It is precisely because they are the easiest that they will be made complicated for major tests. Especially in math, a concept or equation can always be made harder, whether as integrated with other lessons or as a word problem.


Second, in a group, ensure that everyone agrees or disagrees on each step, one at a time. This goes for solving literal algebraic steps, but also for any group project. The plan must be agreed upon, then the execution, and everything in between. Since what comes after depends on what came before, it should be a solid foundation. Likewise, failing to catch an elementary mistake in counting and conditionally filtering the data can lead the entire group down the wrong rabbit hole. So when solving our Problem Set questions, we would always ensure that all steps are manual and independent. There must not only be many groupmates answering the problem, but just as many methods of solving them. This way, every procedure is iron-strong.
