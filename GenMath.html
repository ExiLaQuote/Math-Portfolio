Rationalism

In order to solve the problem set question I was assigned (Logarithms), I reasoned out with our group leader the possible constraints to narrow our strategy. Eventually, we were satisfied with our brainstorming to realize that y needed to be a fraction in order for a quotient equation to result in a larger number than the product of the variables. There were other breakthroughs too that arose from logical conclusions.

Unemployment Graphs


Unemployment, economic inequality, and other socio-political demographic markers of a country all use graphs. In GenMath, we were taught not only to interpret real life graphs, but also sketch models ourselves. I became less averse to even other graphs like scatterplots or charts, since we've graphed problems much harder like exponential decay and inverse functions.


Whether or not I intend to carry over these lessons to the real world doesn't matter, since the course has already shaped my mentality when looking at a graph. What does this y intercept mean? What does it mean when a variable is at 0? What's the range and domain? They all have demographic counterparts and with issues like unemployment, statistics are vital.

GROUPWORK

I loved our problem set group this quarter because they're always so willing to help each other and work together. Even if we were assigned a specific problem to perfect, I recall some days before submission we would huddle around during lunch in order to double check and triple check every problem. And even though I felt like we didn't need to check anymore, we would still find more inaccuracies or details we can improve on. That was the beauty of having more eyes and more minds.

"Andrew..."

IN THE CAR ??

It's not necessarily a time management problem, but rather since I'm good at handling my time, I spent it in the strangest ways possible to make my schedule fit. So yes, I did most of my GenMath in the car, with both laptop and notebook somehow on my lap. My pencil will shake and I will bump up and down, but in the end, I studied math. I solved equations. My friends always think I've gone mad when I say I do this. For me, math is comforting and I shouldn't waste travel time. This was my "innovative" solution to a grave problem of lack of time.

SELF-VERIFIED


There were several instances where I forgot a key concept or fact during the MA or LT. This was a crisis, since a single misunderstanding could ruin 10 points at once. So, what I did was I simply sped up my solving to allow time to verify. To check if I had inverted a function correctly, I would invert it back to the original problem. If it worked, then I was right. Another way I verified was by injecting x values and matching the equation with the graph. It took more time, but it was worth it in the end.

FUTURE CAREER


I found GenMath to be eye-opening because it directly translates to some concept I need to learn in Computer Science. I'm aiming to be a Financial Cybersecurity Engineer, and it's often said that logarithms are mostly used in the real world by technology programmers. We use log base 2 for binary and bytes. Overall I'm thankful for this course and think it's true value has yet to be seen far into the future.
